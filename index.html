Stage 2 of 5 — Core Theory
The four strokes and valve events
Read each section carefully. Use the expandable notes to go deeper.

Intake stroke

The piston moves down. The intake valve opens to allow the air-fuel mixture into the cylinder. The intake valve opens before TDC — this is called intake valve opening (IVO).

Exhaust stroke

The piston moves up. The exhaust valve opens to push burned gases out. The exhaust valve closes after TDC — this is exhaust valve closing (EVC).

Valve overlap

Near TDC between exhaust and intake strokes, both valves are briefly open. This allows exhaust gas momentum to help draw in fresh charge. Overlap is measured in crankshaft degrees.

Why timing matters

Valves open early to allow time for gas flow. Wrong timing reduces power, increases emissions, and can cause engine damage in interference engines.

Why do valves open before TDC/BDC? ▼
Gas takes time to flow. At high engine speeds this becomes critical — if the valve only opened at BDC, not enough mixture would enter. By opening the intake valve before BDC and keeping it open after BDC (using gas inertia), engineers maximise cylinder filling — this is called volumetric efficiency. The exhaust valve opens before BDC on the power stroke to use cylinder pressure to expel gases, slightly sacrificing some power stroke energy for better scavenging.
What is a valve timing diagram? ▼
A valve timing diagram (also called a polar or circular valve timing diagram) shows the crankshaft rotation as a circle (360°) and marks the open/closed periods of each valve as arcs. Intake events are shown in one colour, exhaust in another. The diagram shows exactly when each valve event occurs relative to crankshaft position.
Variable valve timing (VVT) — going further ▼
Modern engines use variable valve timing (VVT) systems — e.g. Honda's VTEC, Toyota's VVT-i — to alter when and how long valves stay open depending on engine load and speed. At low speed/load, conservative timing gives smooth idle and economy. At high speed/load, timing is advanced and lift increased for maximum power. Some systems (e.g. BMW Valvetronic) also vary valve lift directly, reducing the need for a throttle butterfly.
Interference vs non-interference engines ▼
In an interference engine, the piston's travel path overlaps with the valve's open position — if the timing belt or chain breaks, a piston can strike an open valve causing catastrophic damage. In a non-interference engine, there is clearance between the piston and the fully open valve. This is a key reason timing belt replacement intervals are strictly observed.
